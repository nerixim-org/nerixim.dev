## Why this is confusing

Japan's tax system for freelancers involves three overlapping concepts: the qualified invoice system (インボイス制度), withholding tax (源泉徴収), and consumption tax (消費税). Each has its own rules, thresholds, and filing requirements. English-language resources are scarce. The NTA website has some translations, but they assume you already understand the system.

This guide covers what you need to know as a foreign freelancer working in Japan. It's not tax advice — talk to a tax advisor for your specific situation — but it should give you a working understanding of how the pieces fit together.

## The qualified invoice system (インボイス制度)

The qualified invoice system launched in October 2023. Before it, any business could issue invoices that let clients claim consumption tax credits. Now, only registered businesses with a T-number (適格請求書発行事業者登録番号) can issue qualified invoices.

**Why it matters for freelancers:** Corporate clients who pay you want to claim your consumption tax as a credit against their own. If you're not registered, they can't — which means working with you costs them more. Some clients absorb this. Others pass the cost to you through lower rates. A few won't work with unregistered freelancers at all.

**The transitional period:** The system didn't flip overnight. Through September 2026, clients can still claim 80% of the consumption tax credit on invoices from unregistered businesses. From October 2026 to September 2029, that drops to 50%. After September 2029, it goes to zero.

So there's time, but the pressure to register increases each year.

## Registration

Registration is voluntary. You apply through your local tax office (税務署) or through the e-Tax online system.

**What you need:**

- Your My Number (マイナンバー)
- A completed application form (適格請求書発行事業者の登録申請書)
- A registered business address (your home works if you're a sole proprietor / 個人事業主)

Processing takes a few weeks. Once approved, you receive a T-number — a 13-digit number prefixed with "T" (e.g., T1234567890123). This number goes on every qualified invoice you issue.

**What changes after registration:**

- You must charge 10% consumption tax on your invoices
- You must file consumption tax returns (消費税申告) annually
- Your invoices must include specific required fields (covered below)
- Your registration and T-number become publicly searchable on the NTA website

The biggest shift: if you were below the 10-million-yen revenue threshold and exempt from consumption tax, registration means you give up that exemption. You're now a taxable business regardless of revenue.

I registered because I didn't have a choice — my revenue exceeded 10 million yen in the previous fiscal year, so I was already a taxable business. If you're below the threshold, the decision is harder. But if your clients are corporations, most of them will eventually ask for your T-number.

## Withholding tax (源泉徴収)

Withholding tax is separate from the invoice system, but they appear on the same invoices, which causes confusion.

When a Japanese company pays a freelancer for certain types of work — including software development, design, writing, and consulting — they're required to withhold income tax from the payment.

**The rates:**

- 10.21% on amounts up to 1,000,000 yen per payment
- 20.42% on the portion exceeding 1,000,000 yen

This isn't an additional tax. It's your income tax, prepaid by the client on your behalf. When you file your annual return (確定申告), you reconcile these withheld amounts against your actual tax liability. If too much was withheld, you get a refund.

**How it works on an invoice:**

Say your fee is 500,000 yen plus 10% consumption tax:

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Service fee | ¥500,000 |
| Consumption tax (10%) | ¥50,000 |
| Subtotal | ¥550,000 |
| Withholding tax (10.21% of ¥500,000) | -¥51,050 |
| **Payment amount** | **¥498,950** |

Withholding tax is calculated on the service fee before consumption tax, not on the total.

<Callout type="tip">
Keep every withholding tax certificate (源泉徴収票) your clients give you. You need these when filing your annual return. If a client doesn't send one, ask — they're legally required to provide it.
</Callout>

## Consumption tax (消費税)

Consumption tax is Japan's equivalent of VAT/GST. The standard rate is 10% (8% on certain food and beverages).

**The 10-million-yen threshold:**

If your taxable revenue in the "base period" (基準期間 — two fiscal years prior) was under 10 million yen, you're exempt from collecting and remitting consumption tax. Cross that threshold, and you become a taxable business two years later.

Exception: if you register for the invoice system, you become taxable immediately, regardless of revenue.

**Filing methods:**

Once you're taxable, you choose how to calculate your consumption tax liability:

- **Standard method (本則課税):** Track actual consumption tax paid on business expenses and subtract it from consumption tax collected. More paperwork, but potentially lower tax if you have significant expenses.
- **Simplified method (簡易課税):** Available if your base-period revenue was under 50 million yen. Instead of tracking actual expenses, you apply a fixed "deemed purchase ratio" (みなし仕入率) based on your industry. For IT services, this is 50%. Less paperwork.

I use the simplified method. As a software developer, my actual business expenses are low — a computer, some SaaS subscriptions, maybe a coworking space. The deemed purchase ratio of 50% is likely more generous than my real expense ratio, so simplified works in my favor and saves bookkeeping effort.

**The 2-割特例 (20% special measure):**

For freelancers who registered for the invoice system and would otherwise be tax-exempt (revenue under 10 million yen), there's a temporary measure through December 2026. You pay only 20% of the consumption tax you collected, regardless of actual expenses. This is the simplest option if you qualify.

## What your invoice must include

A qualified invoice (適格請求書) must contain:

1. **Your name or business name** and T-number
2. **Invoice date**
3. **Description of services** provided
4. **Amount** broken down by tax rate (10% standard, 8% reduced)
5. **Consumption tax amount** for each rate
6. **Client's name or business name**

Missing any of these fields means the invoice doesn't qualify. The client can't use it for their tax credit.

<Callout type="warning">
If you issue invoices in English, the required fields still apply. Many freelancers use bilingual invoices — English descriptions with Japanese tax fields — to satisfy both their clients' accounting departments and foreign business contacts.
</Callout>

## Common mistakes

**Confusing withholding tax and consumption tax.** They're separate systems. Withholding tax is income tax prepaid by clients. Consumption tax is a sales tax you collect and remit. Both appear on your invoices, but they serve different purposes.

**Not registering for blue form filing.** The 青色申告 (blue form) gives you a 650,000 yen deduction and lets you carry forward losses. There's no reason not to register — you just need to submit the application to your tax office before the filing deadline.

**Forgetting the base period rule.** Consumption tax obligations are based on revenue from two years ago, not current year revenue. Crossing 10 million yen in 2025 means you become taxable in 2027.

**Waiting too long to register for the invoice system.** If your clients are corporations, they'll eventually ask. Better to make a deliberate decision now than scramble when a client sends you a polite email asking for your T-number.

**Not separating business and personal finances.** Open a dedicated bank account for business income. It makes bookkeeping and tax filing vastly simpler. Most accounting software (freee, MoneyForward) can sync with major Japanese banks.
