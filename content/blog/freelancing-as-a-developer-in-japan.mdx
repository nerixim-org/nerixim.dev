## The visa situation

Freelancing in Japan is legal, but the visa system wasn't built for it. There's no "freelancer visa." Most independent developers work on one of these:

- **Engineer/Specialist in Humanities/International Services** — the standard work visa. You can freelance on this, but immigration wants to see contracts, not just "I have clients." Some people get pushback at renewal if their income looks unstable.
- **Business Manager visa** — technically for running a company. Overkill for most solo freelancers, and it comes with office requirements.
- **Spouse visa** — if you're married to a Japanese national, this gives you unrestricted work permission. No employer needed.
- **Permanent residence** — same unrestricted access.

The practical reality: if you're on a standard work visa, keep your contracts documented. Immigration wants to see that you have ongoing work and stable income. A one-page contract with a client's company stamp (hanko) goes a long way.

## Finding clients

In Japan, business relationships run on trust and referrals. Cold outreach works, but slowly.

**What actually works:**

- **Referrals** — by far the most reliable channel. One satisfied client tells another. Japanese business culture moves on personal introductions (紹介).
- **Agent platforms** — sites like Lancers, CrowdWorks, and more specialized IT agent services. The rates are often lower, but they handle contracts and invoicing.
- **Direct outreach** — contacting companies you want to work with. Harder without an existing network, but possible if you can demonstrate specific value.
- **English-speaking market** — remote contracts with overseas companies. The weak yen makes your rates competitive, and you can charge in USD/EUR.

**Japanese business culture quirks:**

Expect detailed specs before work begins. Japanese clients often want thorough written estimates (見積もり) and project plans. Scope changes happen, but they're discussed formally, not casually slacked over.

Meetings tend to be longer and more structured than what you'd see in Western startups. Decisions go through consensus. "I'll check with the team" means exactly that.

## Money

The tax system is manageable once you understand the basics. Here's what matters:

**Withholding tax (源泉徴収):** Clients withhold 10.21% from your invoices (for amounts up to 1 million yen per payment). This isn't an extra tax — it's prepaid income tax. You reconcile it when you file your annual return.

**Blue form filing (青色申告):** Register for this. It gives you a 650,000 yen deduction and lets you depreciate equipment. The paperwork is more involved than white form (白色), but accounting software like freee or MoneyForward handles it.

**Consumption tax (消費税):** If your revenue stays under 10 million yen, you're exempt from charging and remitting consumption tax. Cross that threshold, and you become a taxable business two years later.

**The invoice system (インボイス制度):** Since October 2023, registered businesses can issue "qualified invoices" that let clients claim consumption tax credits. If you're not registered, corporate clients may push back because they can't deduct the tax on your invoices. [I wrote a detailed guide on this](/blog/japan-invoice-system-foreign-freelancer).

**Payment terms:** Japanese companies typically pay on a monthly cycle, often 30-60 days after invoice. Net 30 from end-of-month (月末締め翌月末払い) is common. Budget accordingly.

## The language gap

[PERSONAL: How good is your Japanese? JLPT level? Do you work primarily in Japanese or English with your current clients?]

Working in Japanese as a developer is different from daily conversation. Technical meetings use a mix of Japanese grammar and English loan words. You'll hear フレームワーク (framework), デプロイ (deploy), and プルリクエスト (pull request) in otherwise Japanese sentences.

**Reading and writing matter more than speaking.** Client communication is often written: Slack, email, specification documents. If you can read and write business Japanese, you can work with most Japanese clients even if your spoken Japanese isn't perfect.

**If your Japanese is limited**, the English-speaking client pool in Japan is smaller but real. Foreign startups, international companies with Japan offices, and remote contracts with overseas teams are all options.

Code reviews are the great equalizer. Code is code regardless of what language you discuss it in.

Here's a general stack that works for freelancing in Japan:

- **Accounting:** freee or MoneyForward (both support 確定申告 filing)
- **Invoicing:** freee, Misoca, or manual PDF invoices with required fields
- **Communication:** Slack, Chatwork (still common with Japanese companies), Google Meet
- **Contract management:** mostly PDFs with hanko, some companies use CloudSign or DocuSign
- **Banking:** a Japanese bank account is required. Most clients won't pay to an overseas account.

## Honest downsides

**Isolation.** Japan's developer community exists, but it's smaller and more fragmented than places like Berlin or San Francisco. Meetups happen, but building a professional network takes sustained effort.

**Admin overhead.** Tax filing, invoice management, health insurance, pension — you handle all of it yourself. The national health insurance (国民健康保険) and pension (国民年金) payments are your responsibility and they're not cheap.

**Income instability.** The feast-or-famine cycle hits harder when you're in a country where switching clients is slower. Japanese business relationships are sticky (good for retention, bad for flexibility).

**Language ceiling.** Unless your Japanese is strong, there's a hard limit on the types of clients you can work with and the rates you can command in the domestic market.

## Would I recommend it?

It depends on what you're optimizing for.

If you want a stable income with clear career progression, a full-time position at a Japanese company or a foreign company with a Japan office will serve you better. Benefits, bonuses, visa sponsorship — these aren't nothing.

If you want autonomy, the ability to choose your projects, and the flexibility to work on your own terms — and you're willing to deal with the overhead — freelancing in Japan is viable. The market exists. The tax system is learnable. The quality of life is high.
